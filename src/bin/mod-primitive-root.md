# Primitive Roots mod p

This session asked a question that sits quietly underneath the Baby-Step Giant-Step session: given a prime p, is there always some element whose powers sweep out the entire multiplicative group before repeating, and if so, how do you find one efficiently? The multiplicative group modulo p, written (Z/pZ)*, consists of the residues 1 through p-1 under multiplication mod p. It always has exactly p-1 elements, since every nonzero residue mod a prime is coprime to p. The question of primitive roots is the question of whether this group has a *generator* — a single element from which every other element can be produced by repeated multiplication with itself.

The right way to see this concretely is to watch two different elements behave under repeated multiplication modulo the same prime. Take p = 7, so the group is {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. Multiplying 3 by itself repeatedly gives 3, 2, 6, 4, 5, 1 — all six elements of the group appear before the sequence returns to 1. Multiplying 2 by itself instead gives 2, 4, 1, 2, 4, 1, ... — only three distinct values ever appear, and the cycle is three times shorter than the full group. The element 3 is called a primitive root of 7; the element 2 is not. What distinguishes them is the length of the cycle each one traces out before returning to 1, a quantity worth naming precisely.

## Order, and what a primitive root actually is

The **order** of an element g modulo p is the smallest positive integer k such that g^k is congruent to 1 mod p. For g = 3 and p = 7, the order is 6; for g = 2, the order is 3. A **primitive root** mod p is an element whose order equals p-1, the full size of the group. This matches the two examples exactly: 3 has order 6 = p-1 and is a primitive root, while 2 has order 3 < p-1 and is not.

Before this order can be pinned down efficiently, a structural fact about the group is needed: Lagrange's theorem, encountered earlier when proving Euler's theorem, states that the order of any element of a finite group must divide the size of the group. Applied here, this means the order of any g in (Z/pZ)* must divide p-1. This one fact turns "compute the order of g" from an open-ended search into a search over the (typically small) set of divisors of p-1.

## Characterizing a primitive root without brute force

Directly computing the order of g by trying every exponent from 1 up to p-1 would cost O(p) multiplications per candidate — far too slow for the 64-bit primes this file targets. The fix follows from Lagrange's theorem in a second way. Since the order of g must divide p-1, the order is not p-1 exactly when it divides some *proper* divisor of p-1 instead. Every proper divisor of p-1 in turn divides p-1 divided by at least one of its prime factors — writing p-1 = q1^a1 * q2^a2 * ... * qk^ak for distinct primes qi, any proper divisor of p-1 is missing at least one full power of some qi, and so divides (p-1)/qi for that particular i. This means it is enough to check the handful of exponents (p-1)/qi, one per distinct prime factor of p-1, rather than every divisor of p-1.

$$g \text{ is a primitive root mod } p \iff g^{(p-1)/q} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p} \text{ for every prime } q \text{ dividing } p-1$$

The direction of the check is easy to misstate, and it was misstated once during this session before being corrected: the condition for rejecting g is that some g^((p-1)/q) *equals* 1, not that it fails to equal 1. Equality at any one of these exponents means the order of g divides (p-1)/q, which is strictly smaller than p-1, so g cannot be a primitive root. Only when none of the checks produce 1 is the order forced to be the one divisor of p-1 that survives all of them: p-1 itself.

## Why a primitive root always exists

An algorithm that searches for a primitive root is only correct if one is guaranteed to exist, otherwise the search could run forever having found nothing legitimate. The existence argument is a counting argument, and it is worth walking through slowly because the underlying proof technique — bounding a set from above and comparing it against a set whose size is already known — recurs throughout number theory. The key fact borrowed here is that a polynomial equation x^k ≡ 1 (mod p) has *at most* k solutions modulo a prime p; this is a consequence of p being prime, since Z/pZ is then a field and a nonzero polynomial of degree k over a field has at most k roots.

Suppose, for contradiction, that no element of (Z/pZ)* has order p-1. Then by Lagrange's theorem every one of the p-1 elements has an order that is a proper divisor of p-1, and therefore (by the argument in the previous section) every element satisfies x^((p-1)/qi) ≡ 1 for at least one prime factor qi of p-1. For p = 7, the prime factors of p-1 = 6 are 2 and 3, giving two equations: x^3 ≡ 1 and x^2 ≡ 1. The first has at most 3 solutions and the second has at most 2, so together they can account for at most 5 elements — yet the group has 6. At least one element of the group cannot satisfy either equation, so its order cannot divide 3 or divide 2; by Lagrange's theorem its order must still divide 6, and the only divisor of 6 that is not a divisor of 3 or of 2 is 6 itself. That element has order exactly 6, contradicting the assumption that no such element exists. The same counting argument generalizes to any prime p: an element whose order is exactly p-1 always exists.

## The search algorithm

Putting the characterization and the existence proof together gives a concrete algorithm. First, `factorize(p-1)` produces the distinct prime factors of p-1. This reuses Miller-Rabin for primality testing and Pollard's Rho for splitting composites, both built in earlier sessions, but with a new wrinkle: a single call to Pollard's Rho only guarantees a *nontrivial* factor d of n, not a *prime* factor. An early version of `factorize` in this session pushed d directly into the result without checking whether d itself was prime, and on inputs like 15785 it silently returned composite entries such as 55 = 5 × 11 in what was supposed to be a list of prime factors. The fix was to treat factoring as a recursive splitting process: maintain a stack of numbers still needing to be resolved, and whenever Pollard's Rho returns a factor d of some n on the stack, push both d and n/d back onto the stack instead of accepting d as final. Only when a number on the stack passes the primality test does it get added to the result set. Distinct primes are collected in a HashSet since the recursive splitting can rediscover the same prime factor along different branches (for example when p-1 is divisible by a prime to a higher power).

Second, `primitive_root(p)` searches candidates g = 2, 3, 4, ... testing each with the characterization above, and returns the first one that passes. A second bug surfaced here during the session: the initial implementation never verified that p itself was prime before running the search. Since the entire theory — Lagrange's theorem, the existence proof, even the definition of the group being searched — depends on p being prime, running the search against a composite p produces a value that satisfies the *code's* check but means nothing mathematically. Feeding the function consecutive odd numbers near u64::MAX (most of which are composite) exposed this immediately: the search kept returning g = 2 far more often than chance would suggest, because for many composite inputs the loop structure happened to accept 2 without ever verifying the underlying assumption held. The fix was to run `miller_test(p)` at the top of `primitive_root` and return `None` immediately if p is not prime.

The search starts at g = 2 rather than g = 1 or scanning downward from p-1, and both choices are deliberate rather than arbitrary. g = 1 always has order 1, since 1^1 ≡ 1 trivially, so it can never be a primitive root except in the degenerate case p = 2 (where the group has only one element and that element trivially generates it — a case this implementation explicitly excludes via a guard on p ≤ 2, treating p = 2 as out of scope rather than silently returning a wrong answer). Scanning downward from p-1 fares no better at its starting point: g = p-1 is congruent to -1 mod p, and (-1)^2 ≡ 1 always, so p-1 always has order exactly 2 (except when p = 3, where p-1 = 2 coincides with the group order). Neither direction has a *general* advantage beyond avoiding these two structurally guaranteed failures, because the density of primitive roots among all residues — the fraction φ(p-1)/(p-1) — is a property of the whole set, not of where in the range {2, ..., p-2} one starts looking.

## Complexity

The dominant cost is factoring p-1. Pollard's Rho splits a composite n in expected O(n^(1/4)) time, established in the earlier Pollard's Rho session, and `factorize` may need to invoke it up to log2(p-1) times in the worst case, since the smallest possible prime factor is 2 and so p-1 can have at most log2(p-1) prime factors counted with multiplicity. Each split is also checked with `miller_test`, costing O(log n) per check. Altogether, `factorize(p-1)` costs O((p-1)^(1/4) · log p).

The candidate search contributes a second, smaller term. Each candidate g requires one `mod_exp` call per distinct prime factor of p-1, and there are at most log2(p-1) such factors, so checking one candidate costs O(log^2 p). The number of candidates tried before finding a primitive root is, in expectation, the reciprocal of the density φ(p-1)/(p-1). This quantity is closely related to the sum of reciprocals of primes dividing p-1: writing (p-1)/φ(p-1) as a product of q/(q-1) over the distinct prime factors q of p-1, its logarithm behaves like the sum of 1/q over those same primes, which by the same Mertens'-theorem reasoning used in the Sieve of Eratosthenes session grows no faster than O(log log p) in the worst case. So the candidate search costs O(log log p · log^2 p) in expectation.

$$T(p) = O\!\left((p-1)^{1/4} \log p\right) + O(\log\log p \cdot \log^2 p) = O\!\left(p^{1/4} \log p\right)$$

The first term dominates for every p of practical size, since p^(1/4) eventually outgrows any power of log p. The overall expected cost of `primitive_root(p)` is therefore O(p^(1/4) log p), inherited almost entirely from factoring p-1.

## Worked example

Take p = 7, so p-1 = 6 and factorize(6) = [2, 3]. The search starts at g = 2. Testing the two exponents: (p-1)/2 = 3, and 2^3 mod 7 = 1, so the check fails immediately — 2 is rejected, consistent with the order-3 cycle observed by hand at the very start of the session. Moving to g = 3: (p-1)/2 = 3 gives 3^3 mod 7 = 6, which is not 1, and (p-1)/3 = 2 gives 3^2 mod 7 = 2, also not 1. Both checks pass, so 3 is returned as the primitive root of 7 — matching the direct computation of powers of 3 mod 7 (3, 2, 6, 4, 5, 1) cycling through all six nonzero residues before returning to 1.
