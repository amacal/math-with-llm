# BigInteger Division

## Overview

This session implements division for the arbitrary-precision `BigNumber` type introduced in "Big Integer Arithmetic (Schoolbook)", producing the quotient of two multi-limb integers stored as base 2^64 vectors. The underlying idea is grade-school long division generalized to an enormous base: at each digit position, guess how many times the divisor fits, multiply the guess back out, subtract, and bring down the next digit. What makes this genuinely new, rather than a direct port of the decimal procedure, is that a base-10 student guesses a digit by inspection, while a single base 2^64 digit ranges up to roughly 1.8 times ten to the nineteenth power, making trial and error infeasible. The real content here is the machine that replaces "guess by inspection" with something computable in constant time and provably close to the truth: normalizing the divisor so a cheap estimate becomes reliable, a leading-limb estimate computed with native division, and a short correction loop closing the remaining gap. Karatsuba Multiplication, the most recent prior session in this branch, is not a genuine prerequisite here, since every multiplication this algorithm performs multiplies a single 64-bit digit against the full divisor, never two multi-limb numbers against each other, so there is no asymptotic reason to reach for a subquadratic multiplication algorithm.

## Normalization

The estimation trick this session relies on only works reliably when the divisor's leading limb is large relative to the base. To see why, and to fix it, first establish a free move: scaling both the dividend and the divisor by the same positive factor k does not change the quotient. Writing the division relationship as dividend equals divisor times quotient plus remainder, with the remainder constrained to be at least zero and strictly less than the divisor,

$$a = p \cdot q + r \qquad 0 \le r < p$$

multiplying every term by k preserves the equation exactly, with the remainder now bounded by k times the divisor rather than the divisor itself.

$$k \cdot a = (k \cdot p) \cdot q + (k \cdot r) \qquad 0 \le k \cdot r < k \cdot p$$

The quotient q is untouched; only the remainder scales. This means the divisor's leading limb can be pushed up to any convenient size by choosing k, without changing the answer. The naive guess, halving the base and dividing by the leading limb, only works when that division is exact; truncation in general can leave the scaled leading limb just short of the threshold. The formula actually used, k equal to the base divided by one more than the leading limb, is the normalization factor from Knuth's Algorithm D (The Art of Computer Programming, Volume 2, Section 4.3.1), cited rather than re-derived, since it is a proven fact that this specific choice both clears the half-base threshold and never grows the divisor by an extra limb. Computing it exactly requires representing 2^64 itself, which does not fit in a u64; the implementation casts the leading limb to u128 before adding one, so `base / (leader + 1)` runs entirely in 128-bit arithmetic without overflowing even when the leading limb is u64::MAX.

## Estimating and correcting a digit

With the divisor's leading limb normalized, each digit is produced in two steps: a cheap estimate, then a short verification. The estimate takes only the top two limbs of the current window and the divisor's single leading limb, combines the two window limbs into a u128 value, and divides by the leading limb using ordinary native division — a fixed-size operation independent of how many limbs the divisor or dividend actually have. A theorem from the same source as the normalization factor guarantees this estimate is never smaller than the true digit, and at most two larger. Because the true digit can be as large as base minus one, the raw estimate can reach base, base plus one, or base plus two, none of which fit in a u64; the implementation caps it at u64::MAX before use, since the true digit can never exceed that. The estimate is then verified: multiply it by the entire divisor, and compare the product against the window's actual value using the full ordering already defined for `BigNumber`, not limb counts, since limb count alone does not determine value. If the product exceeds the window, the guess is decremented and the product reduced by one divisor's worth, repeating until it no longer exceeds the window — never more than twice, since the estimate starts at most two above the truth.

## Windowing and the leading-zero pad

Each digit position is extracted from a window built out of the previous leftover, shifted up by one limb, with the next unused dividend limb inserted at the bottom. Before any digit has been produced there is no previous leftover yet, so the very first window must behave as though an implicit leading zero sits above the dividend's own top limb. Two bugs surfaced this session from failing to account for this. When the scaled dividend and divisor ended up with exactly the same number of limbs, computing the window's starting offset as dividend length minus divisor length minus one underflowed a usize subtraction and panicked. When the scaled dividend was exactly one limb longer than the divisor, the first window silently used two real limbs instead of one real limb plus an implicit zero, which broke down once the resulting raw ratio exceeded a single limb's capacity. Both problems disappear once the scaled dividend is unconditionally padded with exactly one leading zero limb before any window is taken; the cases where this padding is not strictly necessary just cost one harmless extra iteration producing a genuine leading zero digit, stripped like any other leading zero.

## Correctness

Every digit produced satisfies the invariant that the window at that step equals the divisor times the digit plus a leftover strictly between zero and the divisor; this is what makes each digit final, since quotient and remainder pairs under that constraint are unique. Normalization guarantees the divisor's leading limb clears half the base without changing the underlying quotient, which is exactly the condition under which the leading-limb estimate is provably never too low and at most two too high. Decrementing while the candidate exceeds the window can only stop once it no longer exceeds the window, at which point the leftover is nonnegative and less than the divisor by construction, so the loop lands on the unique valid digit. The unconditional leading-zero pad makes this apply uniformly to the first digit as well as every subsequent one, regardless of how the dividend's and divisor's limb counts compare.

## Complexity

Let n be the number of limbs in the dividend and m the number in the divisor, with n at least m. The digit-extraction loop runs n minus m times (plus one constant padding iteration), and each iteration does a fixed number of full-divisor multiplications, comparisons, and subtractions, each costing O(m). The total cost is therefore O(m times (n minus m)) — the same shape as schoolbook multiplication's O(n times m), but scaled by the quotient's own length rather than the dividend's.

## Edge cases

A zero divisor returns immediately with no result, checked before any arithmetic begins. A dividend strictly smaller than the divisor returns a quotient of zero without entering the main loop. Dividing a number by itself, or by another number of identical limb length, both fall into the same-length case the leading-zero pad was built to handle. A divisor whose leading limb is already at or above half the base normalizes to a small factor (as low as one) without growing by a full extra limb, and the same padding and estimation logic handles this case with no special branch.

## Worked example

Trace the same idea in base ten for hand verification, since the real base 2^64 case cannot be checked mentally. Divide 91 by 19. The divisor's leading digit is 1, far below half of base ten, so it needs normalizing: the Algorithm D factor is base divided by one more than the leading digit, giving 10 divided by 2, which is 5. Scaling both operands by 5 gives a scaled dividend of 455 and a scaled divisor of 95, whose leading digit, 9, comfortably clears the required threshold of 5. The estimate takes the scaled window's leading two digits, 45, and divides by the scaled divisor's leading digit, 9, giving a raw guess of 5. Verifying this means multiplying it by the full scaled divisor, 95 times 5 equals 475, and comparing against the window, 455; since 475 exceeds 455, the guess overshoots and must be decremented once, to 4, whose product, 95 times 4 equals 380, no longer exceeds 455. The digit is therefore 4, exactly matching 91 divided by 19 in the original, unscaled problem, and the correction loop needed exactly one decrement to get there — illustrating the same at-most-two-decrement bound the real base 2^64 implementation relies on.
