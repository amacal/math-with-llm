# Quadratic Residues & the Legendre Symbol via Euler's Criterion

## Overview

This session asks a concrete question about a fixed odd prime p: given some integer a, does the equation x squared congruent to a modulo p actually have a solution for x not congruent to zero? An integer a for which such an x exists is called a quadratic residue modulo p, and an integer for which no such x exists is called a non-residue. The question looks like it might require searching through every candidate x, but a short experiment with the small prime p equal to 7 already reveals structure worth exploiting: squaring 1 through 6 modulo 7 gives 1, 4, 2, 2, 4, 1, so only three distinct values appear — 1, 2, and 4 — and each appears exactly twice. That repetition is not an accident of this particular prime; it comes from the fact that x and p minus x always square to the same value modulo p, so the six candidates pair up into three mirrored pairs. This session turns that mirroring observation into an exact count of how many residues are quadratic residues, and then proves a genuinely useful test, due to Euler, that decides whether a is a quadratic residue mod p from a single modular exponentiation rather than a search for an explicit square root. That test, together with the notation used to record its output, is called the Legendre symbol, and it was implemented directly in `src/bin/mod-legendre.rs` by reusing the square-and-multiply algorithm from the Modular Exponentiation session rather than reimporting it, per this repo's no-shared-code rule between files. Because proving Euler's Criterion in full draws on five earlier sessions at once — Modular Exponentiation, Modular Inverse, Euler's Theorem & Modular Inverse via Exponentiation, Miller-Rabin Primality Test, and Primitive Roots mod p — while also introducing a genuinely new counting argument and a new proof technique of its own, this session runs toward the longer end of this repository's usual length for a single concept.

## The squaring map is exactly two-to-one

Suppose x squared equals p times some integer q plus a remainder r, so that x squared is congruent to r modulo p by the definition of remainder. Expanding (p minus x) squared and substituting that same relation gives

$$(p-x)^2 = p^2 - 2px + x^2 = p^2 - 2px + (pq + r) = p(p - 2x + q) + r$$

which shows that (p minus x) squared is also congruent to r modulo p, the very same remainder x squared left behind. So every nonzero residue x and its mirror image p minus x always land on the same square, confirming the pairing seen in the p equals 7 experiment.

That mirroring accounts for at least some of the repetition, but it does not yet rule out some other, unrelated pair of residues also colliding under squaring. To close that gap, suppose x squared is congruent to y squared modulo p for two residues x and y, and let z equal x times the modular inverse of y, taken modulo p — an inverse that exists because p is prime and y is nonzero, exactly the guarantee proved in the Modular Inverse session. Squaring z gives z squared equal to x squared times y squared to the power of minus two, since the square of an inverse is the inverse of the square (this follows because y inverse times y equals 1, so squaring both sides gives y inverse squared times y squared equal to 1, meaning y inverse squared really is the inverse of y squared). Substituting x squared congruent to y squared then gives z squared congruent to y squared times y squared to the power of minus two, which collapses to 1 modulo p. The Miller-Rabin Primality Test session already proved that, for a genuine prime p, the equation z squared congruent to 1 modulo p has exactly two solutions, z congruent to 1 and z congruent to minus one — that fact is cited here rather than re-derived. So z is congruent to plus or minus one, and multiplying back by y shows x is congruent to y or x is congruent to minus y modulo p. In other words, the only way two residues can square to the same value is for one to be the mirror image of the other under the x, p minus x pairing already established.

Putting the two directions together, the squaring map on the nonzero residues modulo p is exactly two-to-one: every quadratic residue has precisely two square roots, x and p minus x, and no residue has more or fewer. Since there are p minus 1 nonzero residues in total, this means there are exactly (p minus 1) divided by 2 distinct quadratic residues among 1 through p minus 1, and the same number of non-residues filling out the rest. For p equal to 7 this predicts exactly 3 quadratic residues, matching the observed set 1, 2, 4 from the opening experiment.

## Euler's Criterion

Define, for any a not divisible by p, the quantity w of a as a raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2, taken modulo p. The Euler's Theorem & Modular Inverse via Exponentiation session already proved Fermat's little theorem, that a raised to the power p minus 1 is congruent to 1 modulo p for such a; that fact is cited here, not re-derived. Since w of a squared equals a raised to the power p minus 1, it follows that w of a squared is congruent to 1 modulo p, and by the same two-roots fact used above, w of a must itself be congruent to either 1 or minus one modulo p — there is no third possibility.

One direction of the connection to quadratic residues is immediate. If a is a quadratic residue, so a is congruent to b squared for some b, then substituting gives w of a equal to b raised to the power p minus 1, which is congruent to 1 by Fermat. So every quadratic residue satisfies w of a congruent to 1.

The converse — that every non-residue instead gives w of a congruent to minus one, with no overlap — needs more machinery, supplied here by a primitive root. The Primitive Roots mod p session proved that a genuine prime p always has an element g, called a primitive root, whose order is exactly p minus 1, meaning every nonzero residue modulo p can be written uniquely as g raised to some exponent k between 0 and p minus 2. Consider w of g itself. Since w of g squared is congruent to 1, w of g is congruent to plus or minus one; but if w of g were congruent to 1, then g raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2 would be congruent to 1, meaning the order of g divides (p minus 1) divided by 2, which is strictly smaller than p minus 1 — contradicting that g is a primitive root. So w of g must be congruent to minus one.

The function w is also multiplicative: for any residues a and b, (a times b) raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2 splits as a raised to that power times b raised to that power, so w of (a times b) is congruent to w of a times w of b modulo p. Applying this repeatedly to g multiplied by itself k times gives

$$w(g^k) \equiv w(g)^k \equiv (-1)^k \pmod{p}$$

so w of any power of g is completely determined by the parity of its exponent. Separately, g raised to an even exponent 2j equals (g raised to the power j) squared, which is manifestly a quadratic residue, so every even-exponent power of g is a quadratic residue. There are exactly (p minus 1) divided by 2 even exponents among 0 through p minus 2, exactly matching the total count of quadratic residues established above; since the even-exponent powers already fill every one of those slots, they must be precisely the quadratic residue set, with no residue left over for an odd exponent to also claim and no quadratic residue missing from the even exponents either. Consequently the odd-exponent powers of g are exactly the non-residues, and by the formula above they satisfy w congruent to minus one.

This closes the argument: for any a not divisible by p, w of a is congruent to 1 modulo p exactly when a is a quadratic residue, and congruent to minus one exactly when a is a non-residue. This result is Euler's Criterion, and the quantity w of a — computed as a raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2, modulo p — is given the standard name the Legendre symbol of a and p. Because w is multiplicative, so is the Legendre symbol: the Legendre symbol of (a times b) and p equals the Legendre symbol of a and p times the Legendre symbol of b and p, a one-line consequence of the multiplicativity already shown above, and a genuinely useful shortcut for evaluating the symbol of a large, composite a by combining the symbols of its factors.

Euler's Criterion answers only whether a square root of a exists modulo p; it does not produce that square root itself. Computing an actual x with x squared congruent to a modulo p is a separate, harder algorithm, standardly called Tonelli-Shanks in the literature, and nothing derived in this session attempts it — it remains open as a natural next step building on the machinery developed here.

## Correctness

The implementation's correctness rests entirely on Euler's Criterion just derived: `legendre(n, p)` computes n raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2, modulo p, using the reimplemented square-and-multiply routine, and the theorem guarantees this equals 1 exactly when n is a quadratic residue mod p and equals p minus 1 — the representative of minus one modulo p — exactly when n is a non-residue. The one case Euler's Criterion does not itself cover is n congruent to 0 modulo p, since the entire argument above assumed a lies in the multiplicative group of nonzero residues, where every element has an inverse; zero has no inverse and sits outside that group entirely, so it is neither a quadratic residue nor a non-residue under this framework. The Legendre symbol is defined to be 0 in that case by convention, and the implementation's own test suite confirms `legendre(0, 7)` returns `Some(0)` directly from the underlying exponentiation, since 0 raised to any positive power is 0.

## Complexity

The dominant cost is the single call to modular exponentiation with exponent (p minus 1) divided by 2, whose square-and-multiply loop and O(log p) bound were already proved correct in the Modular Exponentiation session and are reused here without re-derivation. `legendre` itself does only a constant amount of extra work — one subtraction to form the exponent and one modulus check — so its total complexity is the same

$$O(\log p)$$

as the underlying exponentiation call, making this a thin wrapper around already-proven machinery for the complexity argument specifically, even though the theorem connecting that computation to quadratic residues required substantial new derivation.

## Edge cases

Euler's Criterion, and therefore `legendre`, assumes p is an odd prime; the implementation documents this as a precondition with a comment rather than checking it at runtime, matching how other `mod-*` files in this repo treat primality preconditions as the caller's responsibility. Violating that assumption can silently produce a meaningless answer rather than a visible error: when p equals 1 or p equals 2, integer division makes (p minus 1) divided by 2 equal to 0, and the Modular Exponentiation session's own notes already record that an exponent of 0 leaves the accumulated result at its initial value of 1 regardless of the base. So `legendre(0, 2)` returns `Some(1)`, incorrectly suggesting 0 is a quadratic residue mod 2, even though the correct convention (established above) is that the Legendre symbol of 0 is always 0. This was traced through explicitly rather than fixed, since p equal to 2 or 1 already violates the stated precondition and the surrounding theory (the two-roots-of-1 fact, in particular) does not even apply once p stops being an odd prime.

## Worked example

Take p equal to 11. Squaring 1 through 10 modulo 11 gives 1, 4, 9, 5, 3, 3, 5, 9, 4, 1, so the quadratic residues are 1, 3, 4, 5, and 9, and the non-residues are 2, 6, 7, 8, and 10 — five of each, matching the (p minus 1) divided by 2 count predicted above. Take a equal to 7, a non-residue, and verify Euler's Criterion by computing 7 raised to the power 5, modulo 11, since (p minus 1) divided by 2 equals 5 here.

Write 5 in binary as 101, so bits 0 and 2 are set. Start with result equal to 1 and power equal to 7 modulo 11, which is 7. At the first bit, which is set, fold power into result to get result equal to 7, then square power to get 7 times 7 equal to 49, which is 5 modulo 11. At the second bit, which is unset, only square power again, giving 5 times 5 equal to 25, which is 3 modulo 11. At the third bit, which is set, fold power into result to get 7 times 3 equal to 21, which is 10 modulo 11. The loop has now consumed all three bits of the exponent, so the final result is 10.

Since 10 is congruent to minus one modulo 11, Euler's Criterion says 7 should be a non-residue mod 11 — and indeed 7 does not appear in the quadratic residue set 1, 3, 4, 5, 9 computed directly above by squaring. The two independent computations, brute-force squaring and Euler's Criterion via modular exponentiation, agree exactly, confirming the result by hand for a case distinct from the p equal to 7 example used throughout the session's derivation.
