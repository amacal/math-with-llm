# Tonelli-Shanks Algorithm for Modular Square Roots

## Overview

The previous session, on the Legendre symbol, answered a yes-or-no question: given an odd prime p and an integer a not divisible by p, does some x exist with x squared congruent to a modulo p? This session answers the natural follow-up that Euler's Criterion leaves open — given that such an x exists, how do you find it, without a brute-force search through every residue mod p? The algorithm that does this efficiently is Tonelli-Shanks: it starts from a guess that is usually wrong, measures exactly how wrong it is, and then uses a second, carefully chosen ingredient to correct that error step by step until it vanishes. It reuses the decomposition trick from the Miller-Rabin session and leans directly on Euler's Criterion from the Legendre symbol session and Lagrange's theorem from the Primitive Roots session, so those pieces are cited by fact rather than re-proved; only the correction mechanism itself is derived here in full.

## Decomposing p minus 1

The first step mirrors the Miller-Rabin session, where n minus 1 was written as 2 to the power s times d, with d odd, so repeated squaring could search for a nontrivial square root of 1. Here the same peeling-off-powers-of-two move applies to p minus 1, writing

$$p - 1 = Q \cdot 2^S$$

with Q odd, found by dividing p minus 1 by 2 repeatedly and counting the divisions until landing on an odd number. The running example throughout uses p equal to 41, where p minus 1 equals 40, so Q equals 5 and S equals 3.

## The naive guess and its error

A natural first attempt at a square root of a is r, defined as a raised to the power (Q plus 1) divided by 2. Squaring it gives

$$r^2 = a^{Q+1} = a^Q \cdot a$$

so r squared is not generally a itself, but a times a leftover factor t, defined as a raised to the power Q. This relation, r squared equals a times t, is the invariant the rest of the derivation is built around; once t reaches 1, r squared equals a and the search is over. The whole algorithm drives t down to 1 without ever searching candidate values of x directly.

Occasionally the naive guess is already correct: taking a equal to 10 gives r equal to 10 cubed mod 41, which is 16, and 16 squared mod 41 is exactly 10 — no correction needed, since a raised to the power Q happens to already be 1 here, because the order of 10 mod 41 is 5, which divides Q exactly. For a general quadratic residue a this is not guaranteed, and t will usually need a real mechanism for shrinking it.

## Why t always has order a power of two

Euler's Criterion, from the Legendre symbol session, says that since a is a quadratic residue mod p, a raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2 is congruent to 1 mod p. Writing (p minus 1) divided by 2 as Q times 2 to the power (S minus 1), this gives

$$t^{2^{S-1}} = a^{Q \cdot 2^{S-1}} = a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$$

so t raised to that power of two is already 1. By the order-divides-the-exponent fact used with Lagrange's theorem in the Primitive Roots session, the order of t must divide 2 to the power (S minus 1) exactly — never an odd number, never larger. This is the key structural fact enabling an efficient correction: t is always an element whose order is a controlled power of two, never an arbitrary residue.

## A generator for the two-power part of the group

To correct t, the algorithm needs elements of every two-power order, built from a quadratic non-residue z, found by testing small candidates with Euler's Criterion until one gives minus one instead of 1. Define b as z raised to the power Q. Since z is a non-residue, Euler's Criterion gives z raised to the power (p minus 1) divided by 2 congruent to minus one, so b raised to the power 2 to the (S minus 1) is congruent to minus one mod p, not 1, by the same substitution as before. Fermat's little theorem separately guarantees b raised to the full 2 to the power S is congruent to 1. Together these pin down the order of b exactly: it divides 2 to the power S but not 2 to the power (S minus 1), and the only such divisor is 2 to the power S itself. So b has order exactly 2 to the power S — the maximum possible — meaning its powers generate every element whose order is a power of two, including t.

## The order-halving correction

Suppose t currently has order exactly 2 to the power i — found by squaring t repeatedly and counting the squarings needed to reach 1, the same search Miller-Rabin used — and the current generator C has order 2 to the power M, with M at least i plus 1. A cyclic group has exactly one subgroup of each order dividing its own order, so the order-2-to-the-i subgroup inside the group generated by C is itself cyclic, generated by C raised to the power 2 to the power (M minus i). Since t's order is exactly 2 to the power i, t must be an odd power of that generator — an even power would place it in a strictly smaller subgroup. Let h equal C raised to the power 2 to the power (M minus i minus 1), so h squared equals that generator. Multiplying t by h squared adds 1 to t's odd exponent; odd plus 1 is even, so the product drops into the next subgroup down, order at most 2 to the power (i minus 1). This is the whole mechanism: a specific, directly computable power of the current generator is guaranteed to at least halve t's order, without ever computing its discrete logarithm.

Preserving the invariant r squared equals a times t requires updating r alongside t: setting r to r times h and t to t times h squared gives (r times h) squared equal to r squared times h squared, which is a times t times h squared — exactly a times the new, smaller t. The generator updates to h squared for the next round, with M reset to i.

## Correctness

The loop invariant r squared congruent to a times t mod p holds at initialization by construction and is preserved by every update, as shown algebraically above. The loop terminates exactly when t reaches 1, at which point the invariant collapses to r squared congruent to a mod p, meaning r is a genuine square root of a. Termination is guaranteed because M strictly decreases every iteration — the newly found i is always at most M minus 1, since t's order divides 2 to the power (M minus 1) by the same Euler's-Criterion argument re-applied with M standing in for S. Since M starts at S and strictly decreases through non-negative integers, the loop can run at most S times before t is forced to 1.

## Complexity

Locating a non-residue z costs a small expected number of trials, each checked via one Legendre-symbol evaluation costing O(log p); finding S by repeated halving of p minus 1 costs O(log p) as well. Inside the main loop, each iteration first searches for i by squaring t up to S times in the worst case, an O(S) cost, then computes h as a modular exponentiation whose exponent is a power of two of at most S bits, also O(S) via square-and-multiply. Since the loop runs at most S times and each iteration costs O(S), the total work across the whole loop is O(S squared) modular multiplications. Because S is at most log base 2 of p, this gives an overall complexity of

$$O(\log^2 p)$$

modular multiplications, dominating the O(log p) setup cost and vastly outperforming the O(p) brute-force search this session opened with.

## Edge cases

When p is congruent to 3 modulo 4, p minus 1 has exactly one factor of 2, so S equals 1, and the invariant argument above forces t's order to divide 2 to the power 0, meaning t is forced to be 1 immediately — the naive guess is always already correct and the main loop never executes. This is a well-known special-case shortcut some implementations hard-code separately; here it falls out automatically. The implementation also returns None outright for a equal to 0 or a at least p, and for any a that fails the Legendre-symbol check, since Tonelli-Shanks assumes a genuine quadratic residue as input. The subtraction M minus i minus 1 used to compute h never underflows in a correct run, because the invariant guarantees i is always strictly less than M, but this safety depends entirely on the mathematical argument above rather than on any runtime check.

## Worked example

Take p equal to 41, so Q equals 5 and S equals 3, and take a equal to 2. Trial testing finds z equal to 3 is a non-residue, giving b equal to 3 raised to the fifth power mod 41, which is 38. The naive guess is r equal to 2 cubed mod 41, which is 8, and the error is t equal to 2 to the fifth mod 41, which is 32. Squaring t once gives 40, and again gives 1, so exactly two squarings are needed, meaning i equals 2. With M equal to 3 and i equal to 2, h equals b raised to the power 2 to the power (3 minus 2 minus 1), which is 2 to the power 0, so h equals b itself, 38. Updating r gives r times h equal to 8 times 38, which is 304, reducing mod 41 to 17. Updating t gives t times h squared, where h squared is 38 squared mod 41, which is 9, so the new t is 32 times 9, which is 288, reducing mod 41 to 1. Since t has reached 1, the loop stops with r equal to 17, and checking directly, 17 squared is 289, which reduces mod 41 to exactly 2 — confirming 17 is a genuine square root of 2 modulo 41, reached in a single loop iteration.
